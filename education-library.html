Pillar 1

Market Structure

10 readings~115 min

Learning Outcomes — after completing this pillar, candidates should be able to:

  • Distinguish primary from secondary markets and explain their complementary economic roles.
  • Classify order types, describe execution quality metrics (spread, implementation shortfall), and identify when each order form is appropriate.
  • Explain dealer inventory risk and its transmission into bid-ask spreads across volatility regimes.
  • Describe the payoff structure and risk factors of equities, fixed income, credit, FX, and alternatives.
  • Analyse central counterparty clearing, margin dynamics, and the systemic implications of procyclical collateral calls.

1.1 Primary vs secondary markets

Why issuance markets and trading markets solve different financing problems.

8 min readFoundations›

Learning outcomes

  • Primary markets allocate new capital; secondary markets provide liquidity and price signals.
  • Higher secondary-market liquidity lowers the issuer cost of capital via a liquidity premium channel.
  • IPO and bond syndication design affects aftermarket stability.

In primary markets, firms and governments exchange new securities for funding. In secondary markets, investors trade existing claims, setting prices that discipline future issuance. BIS market-functioning work and Federal Reserve primers stress this two-step architecture: allocation first, continuous valuation second. Liquidity premia are economically material: small changes in expected execution cost can move required returns by tens of basis points in stressed regimes.

Go deeper: Federal Reserve education primer, BIS Quarterly Review (2024).

1.2 Order types and execution quality

Limit, market, stop, and iceberg orders in microstructure terms.

9 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Market orders prioritise immediacy; limit orders monetise patience.
  • Execution quality is measured by effective spread and shortfall, not only commissions.
  • Hidden-liquidity tools reduce signalling but can lower displayed depth.

The CFA curriculum treats order choice as a utility trade-off between certainty of execution and certainty of price. Effective spread often exceeds quoted spread in volatile markets, while implementation shortfall captures timing plus impact cost. For large tickets, an apparently cheap explicit-fee venue can be more expensive once market impact is included.

Go deeper: CFA Institute curriculum resources.

1.3 Market makers, dealers, and inventory risk

Why spreads widen when dealer balance-sheet capacity tightens.

9 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Dealers absorb temporary order imbalance and warehouse risk.
  • Inventory and funding constraints transmit directly into wider spreads.
  • Repo conditions can become a macro-finance bottleneck.

Dealer intermediation is balance-sheet intensive. During stress, tighter leverage or funding constraints reduce willingness to intermediate, widening bid-ask spreads and reducing depth. BIS analysis of gilt, Treasury, and corporate bond episodes shows liquidity deterioration can arrive quickly and amplify macro shocks through financing channels.

Go deeper: BIS dealer intermediation analysis.

1.4 Asset classes and payoff structure

Equities, rates, credit, FX, and alternatives as different claims on cash flow risk.

8 min readFoundations›

Learning outcomes

  • Equity is residual claim; debt is contractual claim with priority.
  • Rates products are duration and policy-path instruments.
  • Credit embeds both duration and default/liquidity premia.

A graduate asset-allocation view starts with state contingencies. Equities load on growth and profitability risk; sovereign bonds load on discount-rate risk; credit combines spread, downgrade, and liquidity channels; FX reflects relative macro and policy regimes. Diversification is strongest when risks are structurally distinct, not merely labelled differently.

Go deeper: Federal Reserve market publications.

1.5 Settlement plumbing and systemic resilience

Clearing houses, margin dynamics, and procyclicality.

10 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • CCPs reduce bilateral counterparty risk but concentrate operational risk.
  • Margin calls can be procyclical in volatility spikes.
  • Settlement reliability is a first-order market-quality variable.

Central clearing lowers bilateral default chains but creates concentration in clearing infrastructure. When volatility rises, variation margin and collateral haircuts can force deleveraging. BIS and central-bank papers repeatedly emphasise that market quality depends on settlement capacity as much as on valuation models.

Go deeper: CPMI-IOSCO on CCP resilience.
Pillar 2

Efficient Markets & Active vs Passive

10 readings~110 min

Learning Outcomes — after completing this pillar, candidates should be able to:

  • State the three forms of the EMH and explain the joint-hypothesis constraint (Fama 1970).
  • Interpret SPIVA evidence: 89.5% of US large-cap active mutual funds underperformed the S&P 500 over 15 years (net of fees, survivorship-adjusted; SPIVA 2024).
  • Quantify the arithmetic impact of a 1% annual fee differential on terminal wealth over 20 years.
  • Evaluate behavioural critiques (Shiller, Thaler) and explain why limits to arbitrage allow mispricing to persist.
  • Apply Sharpe's (1991) arithmetic of active management to explain why aggregate active must underperform net of costs.

2.1 Fama (1970) and the joint-hypothesis problem

Why rejecting efficiency requires specifying the expected return model.

9 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • EMH tests jointly assess market efficiency and asset-pricing specification.
  • An anomaly is not proof of irrationality unless risk model misfit is excluded.
  • Implementation costs determine whether abnormal returns are investable.

Fama (1970, Journal of Finance) formalised weak, semi-strong, and strong forms of efficiency and highlighted the joint-hypothesis constraint. A practical implication for portfolio managers: most anomalies should be evaluated net of turnover, impact, and capacity. Gross signals that look statistically interesting can become economically trivial after costs.

Go deeper: Fama (1970).

2.2 SPIVA evidence on manager underperformance

Long-horizon persistence and survivorship realities.

8 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • SPIVA 2024 reports 89.5% of US large-cap active mutual funds underperformed over 15 years.
  • Underperformance rates rise with horizon in most categories.
  • Persistence tests show few top-quartile funds remain top quartile in subsequent periods.

S&P Dow Jones SPIVA scorecards (2024) report that 89.5% of US large-cap active mutual funds underperformed the S&P 500 over 15 years net of fees, with survivorship adjustments included. Survivorship adjustment matters: closure and merger bias can overstate observed skill if ignored. For investors, this turns active selection into a low-base-rate forecasting problem.

Go deeper: SPIVA Scorecard.

2.3 Cost matters: arithmetic of fees

Compounding converts small annual fee differences into large wealth gaps.

7 min readFoundations›

Learning outcomes

  • Net return = gross return − fees − trading costs − tax drag.
  • A 1.0% annual fee gap over 20 years can reduce terminal wealth by roughly 18–20%.
  • Lower-cost implementation raises probability of meeting goals.

If gross return is 7.0%, then 1.20% active all-in cost leaves 5.80%, while 0.09% passive cost leaves 6.91%. Over 20 years on £100,000, that difference compounds into a substantial terminal-wealth gap. This is John Bogle's core argument: costs are one of the few variables investors can directly control.

Go deeper: Vanguard: case for low-cost investing.

2.4 Behavioural critiques: Shiller and Thaler

Limits to arbitrage and slow-moving correction mechanisms.

9 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • Behavioural deviations can persist when arbitrage capital is constrained.
  • Noise-trader risk and career risk limit correction speed.
  • Behavioural evidence complements, rather than replaces, equilibrium models.

Shiller's excess-volatility evidence and Thaler's behavioural finance work document persistent deviations from purely rational benchmarks. Yet these are not free-lunch opportunities: limits to arbitrage, mandate constraints, and drawdown risk can keep mispricing alive longer than managers can stay solvent.

Go deeper: Nobel 2013 scientific background.
Pillar 3

Academic Factor Research

10 readings~120 min

Learning Outcomes — after completing this pillar, candidates should be able to:

  • Derive the CAPM expected-return equation (Sharpe 1964) and identify its principal empirical limitations.
  • Describe the Fama-French 3-factor (1992/1993) and 5-factor (2015) models, defining SMB, HML, RMW, and CMA.
  • Explain momentum evidence from Jegadeesh-Titman (1993) — including the 12% annualised premium in classic 6/6 designs — and associated crash-risk characteristics.
  • Evaluate the quality/profitability factor (Novy-Marx 2013): approximately 2–3% annualised premium in many samples.
  • Assess multifactor integration trade-offs: turnover, crowding, capacity, and the risk that gross backtest premia do not survive implementation costs.

3.1 CAPM foundations (Sharpe 1964)

Mean-variance optimisation under a single systematic risk factor.

9 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Expected excess return is proportional to beta in CAPM.
  • Idiosyncratic risk is not priced in equilibrium with full diversification.
  • Empirical deviations motivated multi-factor extensions.

Sharpe (1964) derives a linear expected-return relation: E[R_i]−R_f = beta_i(E[R_m]−R_f). CAPM remains a benchmark language for cost of equity and performance attribution, even where fit is imperfect. Its strongest contribution is conceptual clarity around priced systematic risk.

Go deeper: Sharpe (1964).

3.2 Fama-French 3-factor model (1992/1993)

Size and value factors as cross-sectional return explainers.

9 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • SMB and HML improved explanatory power beyond market beta.
  • Small and high book-to-market stocks had historically higher average returns.
  • Factor returns are noisy and regime dependent in realised samples.

Fama and French (1992, 1993) documented that beta alone did not explain cross-sectional returns as effectively as a broader factor specification. The model reframed anomalies as possible risk premia or persistent pricing effects.

Go deeper: Fama & French (1992).

3.3 Fama-French 5-factor extension (2015)

Profitability and investment factors reduce unexplained average returns.

8 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • RMW and CMA augment market, size, and value factors.
  • The model explains many return patterns better than FF3 in US data.
  • Value factor redundancy debates remain active in some samples.

Fama-French (2015, JFE) expanded the framework to include profitability and conservative-investment factors. Practical lesson: factor architecture is useful for diagnosis, but allocations must be stress-tested for turnover, crowding, and implementation cost.

Go deeper: Fama & French (2015).

3.4 Nobel Prize 2013 synthesis

Fama, Hansen, and Shiller: efficient pricing and predictable variation coexisting.

8 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Short-run prices are hard to predict consistently.
  • Longer-horizon expected returns vary with valuation states.
  • No single framework resolves all time horizons.

The 2013 Nobel award acknowledged complementary evidence: efficient-market logic at short horizons, and valuation-linked predictability at longer horizons. For investors, this implies humility in tactical forecasting and discipline in strategic policy design.

Go deeper: Nobel Prize press release (2013).

Pillar 4

Investment Strategies

10 readings~120 min

Learning Outcomes — after completing this pillar, candidates should be able to:

  • Evaluate the cost arithmetic case for index investing and interpret tracking difference as a manager-quality metric.
  • Compare lump-sum and DCA strategies using Vanguard evidence (1926–2022): lump-sum outperforms in approximately 68% of periods with a median advantage of approximately 2.2% for 100% equity portfolios.
  • Apply Markowitz mean-variance optimisation principles and explain why unconstrained optimisation is fragile under estimation error.
  • Design rebalancing policy (calendar vs. threshold) accounting for tax wrapper context, turnover costs, and risk-control objectives.
  • Calibrate drawdown expectations using historical crisis data (e.g. 2008: equities approximately −37%, 60/40 approximately −20%).

4.1 Index investing and implementation discipline

Capture market return efficiently by minimising avoidable friction.

8 min readFoundations›

Learning outcomes

  • Low fees and broad diversification improve outcome reliability.
  • Tracking difference is a key manager-quality metric for passive funds.
  • Tax location and rebalancing policy still matter in passive portfolios.

Index investing is not “doing nothing”; it is choosing to harvest market beta with cost control and governance discipline. Vanguard and Bogle research repeatedly show cost reduction has one of the highest certainty-adjusted payoffs available to investors.

Go deeper: Vanguard index investing papers.

4.2 DCA vs lump-sum: probability vs regret

Expected return favours early risk-taking; behaviour may favour staged entry.

9 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Vanguard 1926–2022 analysis finds lump-sum beats 12-month DCA in ~68% of periods.
  • Median advantage is about 2.2% for 100% equity portfolios.
  • DCA can reduce regret risk during volatility spikes.

Because risk premia are generally positive, immediate full investment tends to win more often over long samples. Vanguard's long-horizon data (1926–2022) reports that lump-sum investing beats staged 12-month DCA in about 68% of periods, with a median terminal-wealth advantage near 2.2% for all-equity portfolios. DCA remains a behavioural risk-management tool when loss aversion is high.

Go deeper: Vanguard DCA study.

4.3 Modern Portfolio Theory and efficient frontiers

Markowitz optimisation under estimation error constraints.

8 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • MPT formalises expected return-risk trade-offs.
  • Input error can dominate optimiser output.
  • Robust optimisation and constraints improve real-world stability.

Markowitz (1952) provides the mean-variance framework still used in institutional policy portfolios. In practice, unconstrained optimisation is fragile because expected returns are noisy estimates. Better implementations apply shrinkage, turnover constraints, and broad prior structures.

Go deeper: Markowitz (1952).

4.4 Rebalancing: calendar vs threshold rules

Turnover control while preserving target risk exposures.

7 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • Threshold policies often reduce unnecessary turnover versus strict calendar schedules.
  • Tax wrappers alter optimal implementation.
  • Rebalancing policy is part of risk management, not a clerical task.

Empirical work suggests tolerance bands (e.g. ±5%) can preserve desired risk exposures with fewer trades than fixed quarterly schedules. In ISA/SIPP accounts, tax friction is reduced but spread and impact costs remain. Policy should specify trigger levels, review cadence, and exceptions.

Go deeper: Vanguard rebalancing guidance.
Pillar 5

Personal Finance & UK Wrappers

10 readings~115 min

Learning Outcomes — after completing this pillar, candidates should be able to:

  • Describe ISA (£20,000 annual allowance) and SIPP (£60,000 standard allowance) rules under UK 2024/25 tax law, including taper, MPAA, and carry-forward provisions.
  • Apply lifecycle investing principles to construct a glide-path allocation consistent with human capital and sequence risk.
  • Explain path dependency in decumulation and quantify how early losses amplify withdrawal risk.
  • Apply the 4% rule (Bengen 1994; approximately 95% historical success over 30 years) and evaluate its assumptions, adapting it with modern guardrail frameworks (approximately 3.5% starting rate in lower-yield regimes).
  • Explain UK CGT (£3,000 annual exempt amount) and dividend tax allowances (£500) for 2024/25 and their implications for wrapper sequencing.

5.1 ISA and SIPP rules (UK 2024/25 onwards)

Tax wrappers, contribution limits, and practical sequencing.

8 min readFoundations›

Learning outcomes

  • ISA annual allowance: £20,000, with tax-free growth and withdrawals.
  • SIPP annual allowance: typically £60,000, constrained by relevant earnings, tapering and MPAA where applicable.
  • Dividend allowance £500 and CGT annual exempt amount £3,000 shape taxable-account planning.

A common sequence is: capture employer pension match, maintain emergency liquidity, fund ISA for flexibility, then increase pension contributions for tax relief where access constraints are acceptable. Annual review is essential because policy parameters can change.

Go deeper: HMRC ISA guidance, HMRC pension annual allowance.

5.2 Lifecycle investing and glide paths

Human capital, horizon, and risk capacity in allocation design.

8 min readIntermediate›

Learning outcomes

  • Early career investors typically hold higher equity weights.
  • Glide paths should reflect labour-income risk and retirement proximity.
  • A rule-of-thumb is a starting point, not a substitute for capacity analysis.

Target-date research (including Vanguard glide path studies) supports de-risking toward retirement, but the slope should vary by earnings stability, guaranteed income, and withdrawal needs.

5.3 Sequence risk in decumulation

Early retirement drawdowns can dominate lifetime spending outcomes.

8 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • Same average return can produce very different retirement sustainability.
  • Early negative returns with withdrawals are particularly damaging.
  • Guardrails and spending flexibility improve resilience.

Kitces and related retirement research show path dependency matters as much as long-run mean return once withdrawals begin. A dynamic spending rule (reduce withdrawals after severe drawdowns) generally lowers ruin probability relative to rigid inflation-linked withdrawals.

Go deeper: Kitces on sequence risk.

5.4 4% rule, Bengen, and modern guardrails

From static withdrawal heuristics to adaptive spending frameworks.

9 min readAdvanced›

Learning outcomes

  • Bengen's historical US analysis produced a ~4% starting-rate heuristic.
  • Safe rates vary with valuation, bond yields, fees, and horizon.
  • Flexible spending rules can materially raise sustainability.

Bengen's 1994 work became the canonical retirement-income reference, but subsequent research finds static rules can be too rigid under low-yield regimes. A practical UK framework combines secure income floors, diversified growth assets, and guardrail-based spending adjustments.

Go deeper: Bengen (1994), Kitces retirement income research.
