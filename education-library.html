Pillar 1 — Market Structure

1.1 Markets as information processors

Hayek, price discovery, and capital allocation quality.

10 min readFoundations
  • Prices aggregate dispersed information but only under sufficient competition/liquidity.
  • Operational frictions in clearing and settlement become macro-relevant through capital costs.
  • Theory predicts faster adjustment in deeper, lower-cost venues.

Theoretical prediction: under Hayek (1945), decentralized prices transmit local information more efficiently than centralized planning. Empirical observation: the strength of this signal depends on microstructure quality (depth, tick size, matching rules), not solely on investor rationality. BIS 2024 market function notes show episodes of market stress where impaired intermediation widened financing costs by tens of basis points across sovereign and corporate issuers.

In practice, allocative efficiency and market quality co-move. A one-off dislocation in repo or dealer balance-sheet capacity can affect risk premia, issuance timing, and investment. This is why MSc-level market analysis starts from plumbing before macro narratives.

Go deeper: Hayek (1945), BIS market functioning papers.

1.2 Dark pools, fragmentation, and venue choice economics

When routing improves execution and when it degrades discovery.

13 min readAdvanced
  • Dark execution can reduce footprint for block trades but lowers displayed depth.
  • Fragmentation narrows spreads in normal times and can worsen search costs in stress.
  • Optimal venue choice minimizes total implementation shortfall, not headline fees.

Dark pools and midpoint books emerged to reduce information leakage for large orders. Empirical studies generally show lower immediate impact for institutional blocks, but with a trade-off: less pre-trade transparency can weaken public price discovery if dark volume share rises too far. Theoretical predictions are mixed because adverse selection and inventory constraints vary by venue design.

Fragmentation impact analysis should include all-in execution cost: explicit fee + spread capture + impact + opportunity cost. For large-cap equities, venue competition tends to reduce quoted spreads in quiet regimes; during volatility spikes, hidden-liquidity fill rates fall and routing complexity rises. Academic disagreement remains over the threshold where dark share becomes socially inefficient.

Go deeper: ECB market microstructure notes (2024–2025), BIS papers on market fragmentation.

1.3 Real-time liquidity measurement

From quoted spread to depth elasticity and impact coefficients.

12 min readAdvanced
  • Quoted spread is insufficient: include effective spread and depth resilience metrics.
  • Square-root impact models explain institutional execution costs reasonably well.
  • Liquidity premia vary by regime and can dominate expected alpha for large orders.

Useful dashboard metrics: (1) bid-ask spread, (2) top-of-book depth, (3) order-book slope, (4) Amihud illiquidity, and (5) realized impact per participation bucket. Studies from Amihud (2002) onward support a positive illiquidity premium, though confidence intervals widen in shorter samples and outside US large-cap universes.

Implementation note: use intraday snapshots plus post-trade transaction cost analysis (TCA), not end-of-day proxies alone. Confidence in estimates improves as trade count rises; thin assets require conservative uncertainty bands and stress multipliers.

Go deeper: Amihud (2002), Kyle (1985).

Pillar 2 — Efficient Markets

2.1 EMH in 2025: what survived?

Joint-hypothesis limits, anomaly attenuation, and market impact reality.

11 min readIntermediate
  • Weak/semi-strong efficiency remains hard to beat net of cost at scale.
  • Many anomalies shrink after publication and after realistic execution assumptions.
  • Implementation frictions convert paper alpha into low net alpha.

Theoretical prediction: if information is competitive and arbitrage capital is elastic, abnormal returns should be short-lived. Empirical observation: anomalies exist but are regime-dependent and often attenuate after publication (Harvey, Liu & Zhu, 2016). The practical frontier is not “is EMH true?” but “which inefficiencies survive fees, turnover, and capacity limits?”

For institutional mandates, adding 80 bps gross signal with 110 bps implementation cost destroys value. This is why EMH critique must include impact quantification, not only t-statistics.

Go deeper: Fama (1970), Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016).

2.2 Behavioural anomaly catalogue and EMH critique

Momentum, value crashes, post-earnings drift, lottery demand, and limits to arbitrage.

13 min readAdvanced
  • Anomalies can be behavioural, risk-based, or both; interpretation remains contested.
  • Value and momentum can each suffer multi-year drawdowns.
  • Arbitrage constraints (funding, mandates, career risk) slow correction.

Momentum has strong historical evidence (e.g., 12-1 formation windows), yet can crash in violent reversals. Value historically earns a premium but with long droughts. Post-earnings-announcement drift and underreaction effects persist in some markets, though effect sizes are lower after transaction costs and data-mining adjustments. Academic disagreement persists on whether these are mispricing or compensation for tail exposure.

Methodological standard: report subperiod stability, multiple-testing controls, and economic magnitude. A t-stat without capacity analysis is insufficient for portfolio design.

Go deeper: Carhart (1997), AQR factor insights.

Pillar 3 — Academic Research and Factors

3.1 Multi-factor models beyond CAPM

From FF3 to FF5/6, profitability, investment, and quality overlays.

10 min readIntermediate
  • Single-beta CAPM leaves systematic return variation unexplained.
  • Profitability and investment factors improve cross-sectional fit in many samples.
  • Model choice should match mandate (benchmark-relative vs absolute-return).

Fama-French (2015) added profitability and investment to market-size-value, reducing unexplained average returns in US equities (1963 onward). Yet no model is universal: momentum improves fit for many implementations, while quality and low-volatility overlays may proxy for leverage or sector tilts depending on construction.

Practical implication: treat factors as risk budgets, not immutable laws. Exposures should be monitored with confidence bands because realised betas drift over time.

Go deeper: Fama & French (2015), Dimensional research library.

3.2 Factor crowding, capacity, and fragility

When popular factors compress expected premia.

11 min readAdvanced
  • Crowding can raise correlation across seemingly diversified styles.
  • Capacity limits are strategy-specific and liquidity-regime dependent.
  • Turnover and implementation drag are first-order terms in net alpha.

As assets concentrate in similar signals, entry prices rise and expected spread to fair value shrinks. Capacity pressure is most acute in small-cap, high-turnover, and constrained-short universes. A useful diagnostic is factor implementation shortfall vs paper index returns; sustained gaps often indicate crowding or poor execution quality.

Use comparative performance metrics: information ratio, drawdown severity, turnover-adjusted alpha, and hit-rate by regime. Distinguish statistical significance from investable significance.

Go deeper: JP Morgan factor crowding work, CFA Research Foundation.

Pillar 4 — Strategies and Implementation

4.1 Drawdown case studies and path dependence

2008, 2020, 2022: same long-run return, different investor outcomes.

11 min readAdvanced
  • Maximum drawdown and recovery time shape behavioural survivability.
  • Leverage and liquidity mismatch amplify path risk.
  • Risk controls should be scenario-based, not variance-only.

Case evidence: portfolios with similar annualized return can produce radically different outcomes if losses cluster early or coincide with forced withdrawals. Recovery math is nonlinear: a -50% drawdown requires +100% to breakeven. Therefore institutional strategy reviews should include drawdown depth, duration, and serial-correlation exposure.

Go deeper: Journal of Portfolio Management drawdown literature.

4.2 Rebalancing frequency trade-offs

Quarterly vs annual vs threshold rebalancing under realistic costs.

9 min readIntermediate
  • Frequent rebalancing controls drift but increases trading cost/tax drag.
  • Threshold rules (e.g., ±5%) often outperform calendar-only policies net of cost.
  • Optimal frequency depends on volatility, liquidity, and tax wrapper.

Empirical backtests suggest quarterly rebalancing improves risk control in volatile multi-asset portfolios, but annual or threshold triggers can preserve more net return when costs are material. In taxable accounts, deferral can be beneficial; inside ISA/SIPP wrappers, trading-friction becomes the dominant penalty.

Go deeper: Vanguard rebalancing studies.

Pillar 5 — Personal Finance and Lifecycle Design

5.1 UK tax optimization and wrapper sequencing

ISA, SIPP, GIA, dividend and CGT allowance interactions.

12 min readIntermediate
  • ISA allowance remains £20,000 (2024/25) with tax-free growth/withdrawal.
  • SIPP annual allowance typically £60,000 or relevant earnings limit, with taper/carry-forward rules.
  • Dividend allowance (£500) and CGT annual exempt amount (£3,000) shape GIA optimisation.

Core sequence (most UK households): (1) capture employer pension match, (2) maintain emergency reserve to avoid forced sales, (3) allocate flexible medium-term assets to ISA, (4) direct long-horizon retirement assets into SIPP with tax relief, (5) use GIA for overflow and harvest gains/losses annually. Deviations depend on access constraints, tax band, and objectives.

Limitations: tax policy can change; pension access age and inheritance treatment are policy-sensitive. Reconfirm assumptions yearly against HMRC/FCA updates.

Go deeper: HMRC ISA guidance, HMRC pension allowance rules.

5.2 Pension sequencing and decumulation strategy

Guardrails, annuity-floor decisions, and sequence-risk mitigation.

10 min readAdvanced
  • Fixed real withdrawals can fail under adverse early-retirement paths.
  • Hybrid plans (floor income + growth sleeve) improve spending resilience.
  • State pension timing and DB income materially alter safe withdrawal needs.

Decumulation should be framed as liability matching, not static “4% rule” copying. Guardrail frameworks adjust spending when funded status changes (e.g., cut spending if funded ratio falls below threshold). UK retirees often benefit from combining guaranteed income floors (state pension/annuity/DB) with a growth bucket for discretionary spending.

Methodological note: Monte Carlo confidence intervals should be reported (e.g., probability of 30-year shortfall under 5th/50th/95th percentile paths), not only median outcomes.

Go deeper: Morningstar retirement research.

Morningstar & SPIVA: Active vs Passive Evidence

Evidence snapshot: S&P DJI SPIVA scorecards and Morningstar Active/Passive Barometer consistently show high active underperformance over longer horizons across many categories. The exact percentages vary by category and market regime, but persistence of outperformance is generally weak after fees.

Interpretation standard

Use these numbers as category-level priors, not destiny. Skill may exist, but due diligence must test process repeatability, fee drag, capacity, and after-tax implementation.

Empirical caution: underperformance rates differ by benchmark construction, style box, and fund closures. Always inspect methodology notes before inference.
