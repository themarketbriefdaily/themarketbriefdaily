Every module is structured to move from intuition, to formal framework, to application and risk controls.

Every card begins with a failure or near-failure, then rebuilds the framework using VaR, CVaR, duration/convexity, counterparty risk, and liquidity stress logic.

A1 — LTCM and the illusion of controlled risk

LTCM’s Nobel-powered models looked elegant until Russia defaulted, spreads gapped, and correlations converged toward one. We show why VaR = zσV looked safe in calm data but collapsed under fat tails, funding stress, and 25:1 leverage.

Core tools: VaR, CVaR, leverage, liquidity spiral · Source: Lowenstein, When Genius Failed.

A2 — Barings and short convexity

Nick Leeson’s hidden short-option profile generated small steady gains then catastrophic convex losses. Option payoff geometry and gamma explain why “collecting pennies” can end in institutional ruin.

Story: Barings 1995 · Lens: Greeks and operational control failure.

A3 — Orange County and duration leverage

Robert Citron reached for yield with leveraged inverse floaters; when rates rose, modified duration and convexity transformed a policy mistake into bankruptcy. Duration math is taught through that exact chain.

Formula lens: ΔP/P ≈ -D_modΔy + 0.5C(Δy)².

A4 — AIG CDS and counterparty fragility

AIG wrote roughly $440bn of CDS protection with inadequate capital buffers. We map premium leg vs protection leg, wrong-way collateral calls, and why mark-to-model confidence vanished under stress.

Source: Lewis, The Big Short · Concept: counterparty risk.

A5 — 2008 CDO machine and ratings failure

Mortgage pools were tranched into securities that looked safe in Gaussian correlation assumptions but were fragile in clustered defaults. Tranche waterfalls explain why “AAA” did not mean low economic risk.

Concept stack: securitisation, MBS, CDO, correlation breakdown.

A6 — Repo, basis trades, and modern plumbing risk

Basis traders can be directionally right and still fail if repo haircuts rise. We connect carry, financing, and margin mechanics to crisis-era forced deleveraging dynamics.

Practical use: hedge fund risk committee diagnostics.

This module links principal-agent theory to real corporate outcomes, then maps governance quality into cash flows, discount rates, and terminal value.

B1 — Enron and mark-to-market agency distortion

Enron used opaque mark-to-market assumptions and SPE structures to manufacture earnings while executives extracted private benefit. Principal-agent theory explains why incentive design and audit limits failed together.

Source: McLean, The Smartest Guys in the Room.

B2 — WeWork’s prospectus as governance diagnosis

The S-1 made valuation hubris visible: dual-class control, related-party complexity, and no path to economic profit. We quantify founder-control risk as higher discount rate and lower terminal confidence.

Lens: information asymmetry + agency cost of capital.

B3 — Kodak and short-termism

Kodak invented the digital camera in 1975 yet protected film cash flows instead of re-architecting the business. Edmans’ stakeholder lens frames this as underinvestment in long-run value, not bad luck.

Source: Edmans, Grow the Pie.

B4 — Capital structure in the real world

MM gives a clean benchmark; distress cases show its boundaries. We use debt tax shields, expected distress cost, and refinancing risk to evaluate when leverage creates value versus fragility.

Bridge to LBO and EVA frameworks.

B5 — Private equity incentives: carry, hurdles, and J-curve

Alliance Boots and Debenhams show divergence between operational value creation and financial extraction. We track IRR, TVPI, DPI, carry waterfalls, and vintage effects to separate skill from structure.

Concepts: LBO, carried interest, hurdle rate, vintage year.

From DCF mechanics to narrative risk, this module teaches how discount rates, margins, and terminal assumptions transmit into valuation error.

C1 — Amazon and terminal-value sensitivity

Late-1990s DCFs often valued Amazon near zero because near-term free cash flow was weak and discount rates were high. Damodaran’s narrative-and-numbers framework shows why long runway assumptions can dominate static snapshots.

Formula anchor: EV = ΣFCFF/(1+WACC)^t + TV/(1+WACC)^n.

C2 — Dot-com valuation breakdown

Price-to-eyeballs and price-to-clicks replaced economic discipline. We contrast that period with P/E, EV/EBITDA, and FCF-based valuation guardrails to show why metric choice can institutionalize delusion.

Story: Pets.com, Webvan, and valuation drift.

C3 — Buffett’s Berkshire textile lesson

Buying “cheap” textile assets without durable economics became a classic value trap. Margin of safety is not just low multiples; it needs reinvestment quality and long-run ROIC above WACC.

Source: Buffett annual letters.

C4 — DCF, NPV, IRR, and payback in one decision stack

Capital budgeting choices are ranked with NPV first, IRR second, and payback as a liquidity check. We walk through conflicts between metrics and why opportunity cost sets discount rates.

Decision architecture for research and corporate finance.

C5 — Multiples, EVA, and quality of value creation

Economic Value Added (EVA = NOPAT − WACC×capital) links accounting performance to true value creation. We integrate ROIC-WACC spread with multiple analysis to avoid cosmetic “cheapness.”

Concepts: EVA, ROIC, P/B, value traps.

The module tests efficient markets against institutional reality, survivorship bias, and factor cyclicality, then designs implementable portfolio policy.

D1 — Peter Lynch: skill or survivorship?

Magellan’s 29.2% annualized record is extraordinary, but one outlier does not invalidate EMH alone. We use base-rate reasoning, distribution tails, and false-discovery logic to evaluate manager skill claims.

Source: Lynch and Malkiel debate framing.

D2 — Bill Miller’s streak and collapse

Fifteen years beating the S&P 500 were followed by a 55% drawdown in 2008. The same history can be read as skill persistence or regime-dependent risk loading.

Core idea: distinguish alpha from latent factor bets.

D3 — Medallion and EMH boundary conditions

Renaissance Medallion’s long-run returns challenge simplistic market-efficiency caricatures but also highlight capacity, secrecy, and implementation barriers for ordinary investors.

Takeaway: anomaly existence ≠ broad exploitability.

D4 — Value’s lost decade (2007–2017)

HML underperformance tested institutional patience and career risk tolerance. Factor investors need long horizons, governance discipline, and pre-committed rebalancing to harvest noisy premia.

Concepts: factor cyclicality, benchmark pressure, alpha decay.

D5 — Smart beta, low-vol anomaly, and implementation costs

We compare theory to net results after turnover, capacity, tax, and spread costs. Smart beta is not free alpha; it is a transparent package of factor exposures with execution risk.

Framework: gross-to-net decomposition.

Story-first sequencing: South Sea → dot-com → calendar anomalies → limits to arbitrage → personal decision architecture.

E1 — Newton and Prospect Theory

Newton exited the South Sea Bubble profitably, re-entered near peak, and lost heavily. Prospect Theory and loss aversion explain why intelligence does not immunize investors from crowd-driven re-entry errors.

Quote anchor: “madness of people.”

E2 — Dot-com as a behavioural laboratory

The 1999–2000 collapse (~$6.7tn US equity value erased) illustrates overconfidence, recency, anchoring, and herding in one episode. Shiller’s excess-volatility argument becomes concrete.

Source: Irrational Exuberance.

E3 — January effect and why arbitrage is not frictionless

Calendar anomalies persist partly because implementation frictions, shorting constraints, and career risk can outweigh theoretical alpha. Limits-to-arbitrage is the bridge between EMH and observed mispricing.

Framework: Shleifer & Vishny.

E4 — Noise traders and reflexivity loops

When sentiment trading pushes prices, fundamentals can adapt through financing channels and feedback effects. Reflexivity explains why bubbles can self-reinforce before collapsing.

Concepts: noise trader risk, reflexivity, convex unwind.

E5 — Behavioral control architecture

We convert theory into rules: pre-committed rebalancing, dollar-cost averaging, checklists against sunk-cost and disposition effects, and explicit circle-of-competence boundaries.

Outcome: fewer regret-driven decisions.

Each lesson translates environmental or governance choices into valuation channels: Δcash flow, ΔWACC, and terminal-risk repricing.

F1 — Dieselgate and financial materiality

Volkswagen’s defeat-device scandal wiped roughly 35% off market value in two days and generated $30bn+ in penalties. ESG failure is taught as sudden balance-sheet and discount-rate shock.

Concepts: governance failure, legal liability, carbon risk.

F2 — BP Deepwater and deferred safety cost

Cost-cutting looked accretive until catastrophic tail liability appeared. We map low-probability operational risk into expected value and enterprise risk budgeting.

Lens: expected loss = probability × severity.

F3 — Unilever under Paul Polman

By removing quarterly guidance and emphasizing long-horizon execution, Unilever reframed stakeholder investment as a strategic compounding engine rather than a PR exercise.

Source: Edmans and long-run evidence debates.

F4 — Patagonia and the frontier of ownership design

Yvon Chouinard’s 2022 transfer to a trust/purpose structure tests the outer edge of stakeholder capitalism. We evaluate what this means for governance rights and capital discipline.

Concepts: mission lock, control, accountability.

F5 — Greenwashing diagnostics and impact credibility

DWS and consumer “sustainable” controversies show why labels fail without auditable KPIs. We build a due-diligence checklist for impact intent, measurement, and additionality.

Outcome: separate marketing narrative from measurable impact.

From compounding to retirement drawdown, this module treats household decisions with the same discipline as institutional portfolio management.

G1 — Wealth shocks and financial literacy

Lottery-winner bankruptcy data (often cited near 70% over five years) illustrates that capital without process is fragile. We translate this into budgeting, reserve design, and behavioural controls.

Principle: wealth is a stock; literacy is a system.

G2 — UK pension shift and sequence risk

The move from DB to DC transferred longevity and market risk to individuals. We model sequence-of-returns risk to show why early retirement drawdowns can permanently impair outcomes.

Tools: safe withdrawal rate, guardrails, cash buckets.

G3 — Glide paths, LDI, and asset-liability matching

Institutional ALM principles are adapted for households: liability timing first, asset mix second. We compare target-date glide paths with personalized liability-matching frameworks.

Concepts: duration matching, funded-status mindset.

G4 — Compounding, Rule of 72, and decision hygiene

Compound interest is powerful but path-dependent. We connect Rule of 72 intuition to opportunity-cost thinking, sunk-cost avoidance, and circle-of-competence discipline for long-run behaviour.

Behavioural translation of core finance mathematics.
