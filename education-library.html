CFA-depth, story-first investment education

Each major concept is taught through a real case first, then decoded using theory, formulas, and practical implementation — “story-first” means each lesson opens with a real market or corporate episode before moving into formal tools. Every module is structured to move from intuition, to formal framework, to application and risk controls.

You will master derivative use-cases, pricing intuition, and failure points across options, futures, swaps, volatility products, and structured credit.

A1 — What derivatives are and why they exist

Chicago grain merchants used early futures to survive harvest uncertainty; that same logic powers modern hedging. We separate notional exposure from economic risk and explain why Buffett warned about derivatives while still using them where collateral, terms, and counterparties are controlled.

Lens: hedge vs speculation · forwards/futures/options/swaps · source: Berkshire letters.

A2 — Options: the most misunderstood instrument in finance

Nick Leeson’s short Nikkei structures looked low-vol until the Kobe shock revealed convex downside. We build call/put intuition, Black-Scholes inputs (S, K, T, r, σ), and Greeks as exposure coordinates, not trivia.

Interactive: payoff diagram · Story: Barings 1995.

A3 — Futures and the basis in practice

Metallgesellschaft’s hedge failed not because theory was wrong but because maturity mismatch and liquidity stress broke implementation. Cost-of-carry pricing (F = S × e^(r−q)T), basis risk, and roll yield are tied to operational funding reality.

Interactive: futures P&L + basis sensitivity.

A4 — Swaps: plumbing of global finance

The LIBOR scandal showed benchmark integrity is a balance-sheet risk. We map fixed-floating swaps, currency swaps, total return swaps, and the LIBOR→SOFR transition through legal and valuation mechanics.

Story: LIBOR manipulation by major banks.

A5 — Volatility as an asset class

Volmageddon (2018) destroyed inverse-volatility products because investors sold convex insurance without understanding path dependence. Implied vs realised vol, VIX construction, and volatility risk premium are taught through that unwind.

Story: XIV -96% in one day.

A6 — Structured products and the CDO machine

Through The Big Short narrative, we follow mortgages into tranches and CDO-squared structures where rating assumptions masked correlation risk. Waterfall logic and tranche attachment points explain why “AAA” was fragile.

Interactive: tranche loss waterfall.

You will connect firm objectives to valuation mechanics, then test capital structure, M&A logic, and private-equity incentives against real outcomes.

B1 — The goal of the firm: shareholder vs stakeholder

Friedman’s profit doctrine is tested against Edmans’ evidence that treating stakeholders well can improve long-run shareholder returns. Kodak, Costco, and Patagonia show this as an empirical valuation question, not a slogan contest.

Story stack: Kodak · Costco · Patagonia.

B2 — DCF: the central idea in finance

Amazon in 2000 looked overvalued on near-term cash flows; narrative and numbers had to be coherent over decades. We build FCFF/FCFE, WACC via CAPM, terminal value, and sensitivity discipline to show where model precision ends.

Interactive: DCF calculator · source: Damodaran.

B3 — Relative valuation and multiple traps

The dot-com “price-to-eyeballs” era showed what happens when comparability rules collapse. EV/EBITDA, P/E, P/B, EV/Sales, P/FCF, and CAPE are taught alongside Buffett’s mean-reversion warning from 1999.

Interactive: comparables range builder.

B4 — Capital structure: MM and reality

MM proves irrelevance in frictionless worlds; Toys R Us shows reality where tax shields meet distress and strategic fragility. We connect Proposition I/II, trade-off, and pecking-order theory to debt capacity limits.

Interactive: leverage vs WACC visualiser.

B5 — M&A and the winner’s curse

AOL-Time Warner demonstrates how synergy stories, hubris, and agency incentives can destroy acquirer value. We map deal logic, accretion/dilution, and why target shareholders usually capture gains.

Evidence: acquirer underperformance meta-analyses.

B6 — Private equity: value creation or extraction?

Debenhams and Alliance Boots form a contrast: leverage can either fund operational transformation or transfer value out before fragility appears. LBO mechanics, carry, hurdle rates, and J-curve dynamics are linked to incentives.

Interactive: mini LBO IRR model.

You will map major investor biases, understand why mispricing can persist, and build pre-commitment portfolio rules that reduce behavioral error.

C1 — Why humans are not rational

Newton’s South Sea re-entry loss opens the module: analytical brilliance does not immunise emotion. Prospect Theory (value function, probability weighting, reference dependence) explains why markets can overshoot fundamentals.

Interactive: prospect-theory curve.

C2 — Biases that move markets

The dot-com bubble becomes a full behavioural laboratory: overconfidence, recency, anchoring, herding, disposition effect, and mental accounting. We combine theory with empirical findings (e.g., Barber & Odean).

Source anchor: Shiller, Irrational Exuberance.

C3 — Limits to arbitrage

Royal Dutch/Shell twin-share mispricing persisted despite “obvious” arbitrage because funding risk, noise trader risk, and career risk can outlast capital. Smart money can be right and still go broke first.

Framework: Shleifer & Vishny (1997).

C4 — Behavioural corporate finance

WeWork’s S-1 language and valuation path illustrate CEO overconfidence and investor narrative capture. Biases do not live only in markets; they distort corporate investment and M&A choices.

Story: WeWork governance and valuation collapse.

C5 — Build a portfolio that protects you from you

Odysseus and the Sirens frame pre-commitment: rules beat willpower. IPS design, rebalancing rules, dollar-cost averaging, and guardrails become behavioural risk controls, not just “process.”

Outcome: less regret-driven decision making.

You will separate ESG signal from narrative noise, evaluate evidence quality, and translate transition risk into valuation and capital-allocation decisions.

D1 — ESG definition and materiality

VW Dieselgate turns ESG into finance: governance and environmental failures became a sudden valuation event. We use SASB-style materiality to separate decision-useful ESG from checklist noise.

Interactive: industry materiality matrix.

D2 — ESG and performance evidence

The Friede et al. meta-analysis and Edmans’ employee-satisfaction evidence are examined with factor-adjustment caution. Is ESG alpha real, or factor exposure plus period effects?

Case: Unilever under Paul Polman.

D3 — Greenwashing and credibility crisis

DWS and retail fashion “sustainable” claims show the gap between labels and measurable outcomes. We treat ESG assurance quality as a core risk input, not a compliance afterthought.

Story: H&M Conscious Collection controversy.

D4 — Climate risk is financial risk

Peabody’s bankruptcy and Ørsted’s transition are opposite capital-allocation outcomes under changing policy and energy economics. Physical risk, transition risk, and stranded asset valuation are modelled explicitly.

Interactive: carbon price vs asset write-down.

You will evaluate alternative strategies through fees, liquidity, and factor decomposition, then judge when alternatives improve diversification in practice.

E1 — Hedge funds: strategy map and fee drag

LTCM opens the module: elite models plus leverage can fail when liquidity evaporates and correlations jump. We map strategy buckets, high-water marks, and how 2-and-20 compounds fee drag.

Interactive: fee drag calculator.

E2 — Private markets and the illiquidity debate

Yale’s endowment success shows what skill plus access can do; failed imitators show why governance and horizon matter as much as asset class labels. IRR, TVPI, DPI, and vintage-year effects are compared directly.

Story: Swensen model, and its replication limits.

E3 — Commodities, real estate, and inflation hedging

The Hunt brothers’ silver episode shows that market structure and rule-setters matter as much as thesis. We decompose commodity futures returns (spot + roll + collateral) and compare direct real estate with REIT structures.

Interactive: roll-return decomposition.
