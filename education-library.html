Pillar 1 — Market Structure

1.1 What markets are and why they exist

Capital allocation, Hayek’s knowledge problem, and price discovery in modern finance.

11 min readFoundations
  • Markets solve decentralised information and funding problems simultaneously.
  • Prices coordinate savings, investment, and risk transfer under uncertainty.
  • Primary and secondary markets jointly reduce firms’ cost of capital.

Financial markets exist because modern economies need a continuous mechanism to allocate scarce capital across competing projects. In Hayek (1945), the central challenge is dispersed knowledge: no planner can observe all local conditions, preferences, or production constraints. Market prices compress this fragmented information into tradable signals. A higher copper price, for example, communicates scarcity and demand pressure to miners, manufacturers, and investors at once. In this sense, markets are computational systems for social coordination under uncertainty.

From London coffee-house trading in the late seventeenth century to today’s fragmented electronic venues, the core economic function has remained: move savings to productive use. The primary market channels new capital through equity issuance and bond auctions; the secondary market provides liquidity and continuous valuation. Federal Reserve educational material on primary/secondary markets stresses this complementarity: active secondary trading lowers expected illiquidity discounts, which lowers issuance costs for firms and sovereigns.

Capital allocation quality can be split into allocative efficiency and operational efficiency. Allocative efficiency asks whether capital reaches the highest net-present-value opportunities; operational efficiency asks how cheaply and reliably trades clear. BIS market structure reviews show that transaction cost frictions, clearing failures, and settlement risk directly impair both. Efficient operations are therefore not merely plumbing; they shape real investment and growth outcomes.

Markets also perform risk transfer. Equity shifts business risk to investors with long horizons; bonds transfer duration and default risk; derivatives unbundle and redistribute specific exposures. CFA curriculum framing treats this as a welfare function: households and institutions can hold portfolios closer to their desired consumption paths. Price discovery emerges from this same process. Competing beliefs are converted into quotes and trades, and equilibrium prices become probability-weighted expectations about future cash flows and discount rates.

Go deeper: Hayek (1945), Federal Reserve primers, BIS market structure publications.

1.2 Asset classes: a rigorous taxonomy

Equities, bonds, commodities, FX and alternatives with evidence on return, risk and correlation.

12 min readIntermediate
  • Equities delivered roughly 5% real annualised returns over 1900–2020 in DMS data.
  • Bonds supply duration and income but face inflation/credit regime risk.
  • Commodities, FX and alternatives diversify specific macro shocks.

Dimson, Marsh and Staunton’s long-run dataset across 20+ countries reports global equities near 5% real annualised return over roughly 120 years, with major dispersion by country and period. The equity risk premium exists because shareholders absorb residual business risk and earnings cyclicality. Volatility and drawdown depth are the price of that premium: twentieth-century equity bear markets frequently exceeded 30%, and multi-year recoveries were common.

Bonds are best decomposed into rate risk (duration) and credit risk (default/liquidity spread). Term structure theory links longer maturity bonds to higher sensitivity to yield changes; a duration of 8 implies roughly an 8% price drop for a 1 percentage-point rise in yields. Credit spreads compensate default and downgrade uncertainty. Government bonds historically provide recession hedging in disinflationary shocks, but that relationship weakened in inflationary episodes such as 2021–2022.

Commodity returns combine spot movement, collateral return, and roll yield. Convenience yield and inventory dynamics explain backwardation/contango states: when inventories are tight, near contracts can trade at premiums. Commodities can hedge inflation shocks, but expected real returns are lower and more cyclical than equities. FX is a relative-valuation market; purchasing power parity is a long-horizon anchor, while carry trade premia (borrowing low-rate, investing high-rate currencies) persist with crash risk.

Alternatives include private equity, infrastructure, real estate and hedge-fund strategies. Illiquidity premium theory suggests investors can earn excess return for locking capital, but empirical decomposition often attributes part of private market outperformance to leverage, sector mix and stale pricing. Portfolio construction should therefore use transparent assumptions on liquidity, valuation frequency and correlation instability rather than headline IRRs alone.

Go deeper: Dimson, Marsh & Staunton Yearbook, CFA asset allocation readings.

1.3 Market microstructure: how prices actually form

Order types, spreads, Kyle’s adverse selection model, dark pools and HFT.

11 min readAdvanced
  • Bid-ask spreads compensate inventory risk, order-processing cost, and adverse selection.
  • Kyle (1985) formalised price impact from informed order flow.
  • Execution quality can dominate expected alpha for large trades.

Microstructure focuses on how trading rules convert orders into prices. A market order demands immediacy and pays the spread; a limit order supplies liquidity and bears fill risk and adverse selection. Dealers or liquidity providers quote two-sided prices to earn spread revenue while managing inventory and information risk. Informed flow pushes quoted spreads wider because uninformed liquidity providers need protection from trading against superior information.

Kyle (1985) models linear price impact: price change equals impact coefficient times signed order flow. Higher private information and lower depth raise impact; large investors therefore split orders to reduce footprint. Amihud (2002) proposes an illiquidity proxy using absolute return per unit volume; empirically, higher Amihud illiquidity predicts higher expected returns as compensation for trading frictions.

Fragmented markets add routing complexity. Dark pools can lower displayed impact for large blocks but reduce pre-trade transparency. HFT has narrowed spreads in many instruments, yet concerns remain around fleeting liquidity and latency races. For institutional investors, implementation shortfall, not model alpha, often determines realised performance. Execution policy, participation limits, and venue selection are core risk controls.

Go deeper: Kyle (1985), Amihud (2002).

Pillar 2 — Efficient Markets

2.1 Efficient Market Hypothesis: theory and evidence

Fama (1970), random walk evidence, anomalies and the modern consensus.

12 min readAdvanced
  • EMH has weak, semi-strong, and strong forms with different test implications.
  • Joint-hypothesis and Roll critiques limit “pure” efficiency tests.
  • Anomalies exist, but net exploitable alpha remains scarce after costs.

Fama (1970) defines efficient markets as those in which prices fully reflect available information. Weak-form efficiency rejects technical rules based only on past prices; semi-strong form implies public news is incorporated rapidly; strong form extends to private information. In practice, evidence most strongly supports weak and semi-strong efficiency in liquid markets.

Testing EMH is complicated by the joint-hypothesis problem: every test of efficiency is also a test of the asset-pricing model used to define “fair value”. Roll’s critique formalises this point: because the true market portfolio is unobservable, rejection of CAPM-based tests may reflect benchmark misspecification rather than inefficiency.

Still, anomalies are robust in data. Basu (1977) documented a value effect via low P/E portfolios; Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) found medium-term momentum profits; calendar effects such as January seasonality appear in some subperiods. The modern consensus is nuanced: markets are not perfectly efficient, but they are sufficiently competitive that persistent abnormal returns net of fees and trading frictions are difficult to harvest at scale.

2.2 SPIVA evidence: what happens to active managers

Long-horizon underperformance, survivorship bias, and persistence breakdown.

10 min readIntermediate
  • SPIVA frequently reports ~65% US large-cap underperformance at 1 year.
  • At 15 years, underperformance rates are commonly above 90%.
  • Persistence studies show few repeat winners after costs.

SPIVA scorecards consistently find most active managers lag benchmarks net of fees. Recent scorecards have shown around 65% of US large-cap managers underperforming over one year and over 90% underperforming over 15 years. Similar patterns appear internationally and in many fixed-income categories, though the exact percentages vary by regime and benchmark definition.

The key methodological strength is adjustment for survivorship. Funds that close or merge are not simply dropped from the sample, reducing the upward bias found in self-selected return databases. Carhart (1997) showed apparent persistence in top-quartile funds largely reflects momentum exposure and expenses; genuine skill persistence is weak once these are controlled.

Fama and French (2010) aggregate active returns and conclude that before costs, active as a group roughly earns market returns; after costs, aggregate alpha is negative. This is arithmetic: active investors collectively are the market portfolio before fees, so fees and frictions must push average net performance below passive alternatives.

Go deeper: SPIVA reports, Carhart (1997), Fama & French (2010).

2.3 Behavioural finance: where markets fail

Prospect theory, excess volatility, equity premium puzzle and limits of arbitrage.

11 min readAdvanced
  • Losses are weighted about 2.25x gains in prospect-theory estimates.
  • Shiller (1981) found equity prices more volatile than dividends justify.
  • Limits to arbitrage prevent immediate correction of all mispricings.

Kahneman and Tversky’s prospect theory replaces expected-utility assumptions with empirically observed reference dependence and loss aversion. A common estimate places the loss-aversion coefficient around 2.25: losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains help. This can explain momentum crashes, panic selling, and path-dependent risk appetite.

Shiller (1981) showed observed equity volatility exceeded levels implied by discounted dividend fundamentals under simple rational models. Mehra and Prescott (1985) identified the equity premium puzzle: historical excess returns over risk-free assets appear too high for plausible representative-agent risk aversion. Barber and Odean document overconfidence and turnover effects in retail trading, while disposition effects show investors realise gains too early and hold losses too long.

Shleifer and Vishny (1997) explain why arbitrage is limited in practice: capital constraints, funding risk, and horizon mismatch can force rational traders to unwind before convergence. Behavioural finance therefore complements EMH. Prices are disciplined by competition, but not frictionless; deviations can persist when arbitrage capital is scarce or constrained.

Go deeper: Shiller (1981), Mehra & Prescott (1985), Barber & Odean, Shleifer & Vishny (1997).

Pillar 3 — Academic Research

3.1 Sharpe: CAPM and the Sharpe Ratio

CAPM logic, SML predictions, and performance-measure caveats.

10 min readAdvanced
  • CAPM predicts expected return is linear in beta via the security market line.
  • Empirically, SML is flatter than CAPM predicts in many datasets.
  • Sharpe Ratio is useful but blind to skew/tail risk.

Markowitz (1952) established mean-variance optimisation, showing that rational investors choose portfolios on the efficient frontier. Sharpe (1964) extends this to equilibrium with a risk-free asset and homogeneous expectations. CAPM predicts: E[Ri] = Rf + βi(E[Rm] - Rf). The intercept is the risk-free rate; the slope is the market risk premium.

Fama and MacBeth (1973) and later evidence find the security market line often flatter than predicted: low-beta assets can earn higher-than-CAPM returns, while high-beta assets underdeliver. This pushed research toward multifactor models.

The Sharpe Ratio, (Rp-Rf)/σp, remains a core risk-adjusted metric. It is scaleable and intuitive, but assumes volatility is a sufficient risk proxy and can overstate quality for strategies with negative skew or hidden tail exposure. Treynor Ratio isolates systematic risk, and Jensen’s alpha measures excess return relative to model-predicted return.

Go deeper: Sharpe (1964), Fama & MacBeth (1973).

3.2 Fama-French: from anomalies to models

SMB/HML construction, five-factor extension, and factor-zoo caution.

12 min readAdvanced
  • Banz (1981) and Basu (1977) motivated size and value factors.
  • Three-factor model adds SMB and HML to market beta.
  • Five-factor model adds profitability (RMW) and investment (CMA).

Evidence that small-cap and value stocks earned excess returns challenged single-factor CAPM. Fama and French (1992, 1993) formalised this via SMB (small minus big) and HML (high book-to-market minus low). Portfolios are built by sorting stocks on size and value, then combining long-short legs to isolate each characteristic.

The five-factor model (2015) adds RMW (robust minus weak profitability) and CMA (conservative minus aggressive investment), reflecting empirical links between operating profitability, asset growth, and expected returns. International tests (Fama and French, 2017) show broad support, but factor premia vary by region and era.

Harvey, Liu and Zhu (2016) warn of p-hacking in the “factor zoo”: with hundreds of tested signals, many t-stats can appear significant by chance. Practical factor investing therefore requires robustness checks, implementation-cost adjustment, and out-of-sample validation.

3.3 Factor landscape today

Momentum, quality, low volatility, cyclicality and crowding risk.

10 min readIntermediate
  • Momentum historically delivered roughly 8–10% annual long-short premia in US samples.
  • Quality/profitability factors are persistent across many markets.
  • Factor diversification can reduce drawdown concentration.

Momentum (Jegadeesh and Titman, 1993) remains among the strongest anomalies but suffers episodic crashes, especially during sharp regime reversals. Profitability/quality factors (Novy-Marx, 2013) link higher gross profitability to higher expected returns. Low-volatility anomalies challenge CAPM by showing lower-beta portfolios can match or beat broad-market returns on a risk-adjusted basis.

Factors are cyclical. Value underperformed growth for much of 2007–2020, then partially recovered as rates rose. AQR and other practitioners emphasise multi-factor diversification because value and momentum are often negatively correlated, reducing cycle-specific pain. Crowding and capacity constraints matter: if too much capital chases a factor, future premia compress and unwind risk rises.

Implementation in ETFs and smart beta products introduces tracking error, turnover and tax trade-offs. Investors should evaluate factor products on methodology transparency, rebalancing rules, capacity, and live implementation cost, not headline backtests alone.

Go deeper: Novy-Marx (2013), AQR factor research.

Pillar 4 — Strategies

4.1 Passive investing: the evidence-based default

Sharpe’s arithmetic, index mechanics, and concentration caveats.

9 min readFoundations
  • Sharpe (1991): before costs, active equals passive in aggregate; after costs, active must underperform.
  • SPIVA long-horizon data shows 90%+ underperformance in many categories.
  • Passive is not risk-free: concentration and valuation risk remain.

Passive investing tracks market-cap indices with minimal turnover and lower fees. Sharpe’s arithmetic of active management (1991) is decisive: because active investors collectively hold the market portfolio before costs, their weighted average net return must be below market after costs.

Index replication uses full replication or stratified sampling. Tracking error is the implementation metric: lower is generally better for broad beta exposure. Critiques are valid—cap-weighting can concentrate in mega-caps, and passive vehicles still transmit market drawdowns—but evidence suggests low-cost broad indexing is a strong default for long-horizon investors.

Go deeper: Sharpe (1991), Vanguard indexing research.

4.2 Factor investing: systematic tilts

Risk-premium versus behavioural explanations and practical implementation.

10 min readIntermediate
  • Factor premia likely reflect both risk compensation and behavioural mispricing.
  • Long-only tilts are implementation-friendly but weaker than long-short purity.
  • Timing factors has limited reliable evidence.

Factor investing systematically tilts portfolios toward characteristics linked to excess returns. Rational stories emphasise macro and distress risk; behavioural stories emphasise investor underreaction, overreaction, and institutional constraints. Empirically, both likely contribute depending on factor and regime.

Long-only implementations are accessible in mutual funds/ETFs but dilute premia and raise market beta overlap. Long-short implementations isolate factors more cleanly but add leverage, financing, and governance complexity. Evidence from AQR and Dimensional supports diversified multi-factor exposure rather than single-factor concentration.

Go deeper: AQR papers, Dimensional research.

4.3 Portfolio construction: from theory to practice

Markowitz, Black-Litterman, risk parity, and rebalancing evidence.

10 min readAdvanced
  • Mean-variance optimisation is highly sensitive to expected-return inputs.
  • Black-Litterman stabilises allocations by blending prior equilibrium and investor views.
  • Rebalancing trades small return drag for better risk control.

Markowitz optimisation is elegant but unstable in practice: small input changes can produce extreme weights. Black-Litterman addresses this by starting from market-implied equilibrium returns and introducing calibrated active views. Risk parity shifts focus from capital weights to risk-contribution weights, often levering lower-volatility assets to balance risk budgets.

Rebalancing evidence suggests periodic or threshold-based methods reduce drift and maintain intended risk exposure. During crises, cross-asset correlations can rise toward one, so diversification weakens when most needed; nonetheless, diversified portfolios typically remain less volatile than concentrated exposures over full cycles.

Go deeper: Markowitz (1952), Black-Litterman resources.

4.4 DCA, buy-and-hold and behavioural structure

Lump sum vs DCA evidence and the cost of market timing mistakes.

9 min readFoundations
  • Vanguard analysis: lump sum outperformed DCA about two-thirds of periods.
  • DCA still helps behaviour and regret management for many households.
  • Missing a few best market days can severely reduce long-run returns.

When capital is available upfront, expected-return logic favours lump-sum investment because risky assets have positive long-run expected excess returns. Vanguard’s comparative work found lump sum beat DCA in roughly two-thirds of historical windows across major markets.

For ongoing savers, DCA is natural because income arrives over time. Behaviourally, rules-based investing lowers the temptation to time headlines. JP Morgan’s “best days” illustrations show that being out of markets during short rebound bursts can dramatically cut terminal wealth. The lesson is less about prediction and more about staying invested with an implementable plan.

Go deeper: Vanguard DCA research, JP Morgan market timing studies.

Pillar 5 — Personal Finance by Life Stage

5.1 Framework: horizon, risk and goals

Human capital, risk capacity vs tolerance, and goal-based allocation.

10 min readFoundations
  • Human capital is often the largest asset for younger earners.
  • Risk capacity (can take risk) differs from risk tolerance (willing to take risk).
  • Goals and liabilities should anchor strategic allocation.

Bodie, Merton and Samuelson (1992) frame household finance as total wealth management: financial capital plus human capital. Early-career human capital behaves somewhat bond-like for stable professions, supporting higher equity allocations in financial portfolios. But sector cyclicality, job security and debt burdens can make human capital equity-like, reducing safe risk capacity.

Risk capacity is objective (income stability, emergency reserves, time horizon); risk tolerance is psychological. A sound plan needs both. Goal-based investing maps assets to liabilities: short-term goals to low-volatility assets, long-term goals to growth assets. Age is a useful heuristic, not a complete policy rule.

5.2 Early to mid-career (20s–40s): accumulation

Compounding maths, UK wrappers, emergency funds and behaviour design.

9 min readIntermediate
  • £500/month at 7% for 30 years is ~£566k; starting 10 years later gives ~£245k.
  • Use employer pension match first, then ISA/LISA/SIPP sequencing by goal.
  • Emergency funds of 3–6 months reduce forced selling risk.

The dominant variable in wealth accumulation is time invested, not tactical precision. Using a 7% nominal return assumption, £500 monthly for 30 years compounds to roughly £566,000; delaying by 10 years cuts it to about £245,000. Early consistency dominates late intensity.

UK implementation: retain pension auto-enrolment, capture full employer match, and use Stocks & Shares ISA allowance for medium-term flexibility. Lifetime ISA can support first-home goals subject to product rules and penalties. Avoid leakage behaviours such as pension cash-outs on job changes and static contribution rates despite salary growth.

Go deeper: HMRC ISA guidance, MoneyHelper pensions guidance.

5.3 Approaching retirement (50s–65+): decumulation

Sequence risk, 4% rule, glide paths and UK drawdown decisions.

10 min readAdvanced
  • Early negative returns in retirement can permanently impair withdrawal sustainability.
  • Bengen’s 4% rule is a historical heuristic, not a guarantee.
  • Flexible drawdown rules improve resilience versus fixed real withdrawals.

Sequence-of-returns risk means return order matters once withdrawals begin. Kitces and related retirement research shows poor first-decade returns can overwhelm average long-run return assumptions. Bengen (1994) found a 4% initial withdrawal (inflation adjusted) survived historical US data in many windows, but global evidence and current valuations suggest lower or flexible rates may be prudent for some retirees.

UK retirees must integrate State Pension timing, defined-benefit income floors, annuity pricing, and flexi-access drawdown rules. Glide paths can reduce equity risk near retirement, but excessive de-risking raises inflation risk over long retirements. Dynamic spending guardrails are often more robust than fixed withdrawal policies.

Go deeper: Kitces retirement research, Bengen (1994).

5.4 UK tax wrappers: ISA, SIPP and GIA sequencing

2024/25 limits, relief mechanics and practical wrapper prioritisation.

9 min readIntermediate
  • ISA allowance: £20,000 in 2024/25.
  • SIPP annual allowance: £60,000 or 100% of earnings (whichever lower, with rules).
  • Sequencing depends on goal horizon, access needs, and tax band.

ISA wrappers provide tax-free growth and withdrawals with high flexibility. Stocks & Shares ISA, Cash ISA, Innovative Finance ISA, and Lifetime ISA serve different needs but share the £20,000 annual cap (with product-specific constraints). SIPPs provide tax relief at contribution and tax-deferred growth, with annual allowance generally £60,000 (or earnings-based lower limit), subject to tapering and carry-forward rules where eligible.

Since Lifetime Allowance abolition (from 2023/24 reforms), planning focus has shifted toward annual limits and withdrawal taxation. A common sequencing framework is: capture pension match, build emergency liquidity, fund ISA for flexibility, then maximise SIPP for long-horizon retirement tax efficiency. General Investment Accounts fill overflow capacity and can be managed via annual CGT/income allowances and tax-loss harvesting. IHT treatment of pensions remains policy-sensitive and should be reviewed as rules evolve.

Go deeper: HMRC pension annual allowance, HMRC ISA rules.
