The Unanchored Central Banker: Why Inflation May Be Structurally Higher for a Decade

Market Brief Daily • May 28, 2026

Disclosure

For informational purposes only. Not investment advice. Forecasts are uncertain and positions may change without notice. See disclaimer.

For most of the last three decades, low and stable inflation was treated as a permanent feature of the economic landscape — a problem that modern central banking had essentially solved. Inflation targeting, independent central banks, and credible forward guidance were credited with taming the price instability of the 1970s. The story was reassuring, and it was widely believed.

It may also have been largely wrong. A growing body of work — most forcefully Charles Goodhart and Manoj Pradhan's The Great Demographic Reversal and the broader argument around what they call the unanchored central banker — suggests the disinflation of 1990 to 2020 was not primarily a victory of policy. It was a one-off structural windfall: the largest positive labour-supply shock in human history, arriving at the exact moment economists were congratulating themselves. And that windfall is now going into reverse.

This brief lays out the demographic thesis, why the Phillips curve may be waking from a long coma, how Baumol's cost disease and structurally tight housing reinforce the pressure, the role of geopolitical supply shocks as a catalyst, and why fiscal dominance could leave central banks fighting a war they are no longer equipped to win.

The Core Thesis: Disinflation Was a Gift, Not a Skill

Between roughly 1990 and 2018, the effective global labour force available to the world's traded-goods economy did not just grow — it roughly doubled. Three forces converged at once. China re-entered the world economy and joined the World Trade Organization in 2001, adding hundreds of millions of workers to global supply chains. The former Soviet bloc in Eastern Europe integrated into Western production networks. And female labour force participation rose sharply across the developed world. Layer in the working-age cohorts of the post-war baby boom still in their prime earning years, and the result was an unprecedented surge in the supply of workers relative to the supply of capital.

When labour is abundant and globally contestable, wage bargaining power collapses. A factory worker in Ohio or Stuttgart was suddenly competing, at the margin, with a worker in Shenzhen earning a fraction of the wage. That competition held down wages, held down the prices of manufactured goods, and held down inflation across the developed world — regardless of what any central banker did. Deflationary pressure was, in effect, imported. Goodhart and Pradhan's central claim is that this was the dominant disinflationary force of the era, dwarfing the contribution of monetary policy. Central banks took the credit; demographics did the work.

The uncomfortable corollary is simple. If a structural tailwind delivered the low inflation of the past, then the reversal of that tailwind should deliver structurally higher inflation in the future — and no amount of inflation-targeting credibility can fully offset a shrinking global supply of workers.

The Great Demographic Reversal

The reversal is already underway, and its mechanics are demographic rather than cyclical, which is what makes them so durable. China — the engine of the disinflationary era — is now ageing rapidly. Its working-age population has peaked and is declining, and its old-age dependency ratio is climbing fast. To grasp the scale of what is unwinding, consider Goodhart and Pradhan's striking observation: between 1990 and 2021, the increase in working-age Chinese people exceeded the combined working-age populations of the United States and Europe by a factor of several times. That single country supplied a labour shock the developed world could never have generated on its own. It is not coming back.

Across the developed world and increasingly in China, the dependency ratio is rising — more retirees, fewer workers to support them. Older people consume but do not produce. They draw down savings, demand healthcare and services, and shrink the pool of workers available to meet that demand. This is inherently inflationary: demand holds up while the supply of labour to satisfy it contracts. The same demographic force that suppressed wages on the way up will support them on the way down. Labour scarcity restores the bargaining power that globalisation stripped away.

India and parts of Africa still have growing working-age populations, and optimists point to them as a replacement engine. But India's integration into global manufacturing supply chains is nowhere near the scale or speed of China's, and the infrastructure, institutions, and capital deepening required take decades. The demographic dividend is not fungible on the timeline that matters for the next ten years of inflation.

The Return of the Phillips Curve

For much of the 2010s, economists debated whether the Phillips curve — the historical relationship between low unemployment and rising wages and prices — was dead. Unemployment fell to multi-decade lows and inflation stubbornly refused to appear. The curve looked flat, even broken.

The demographic thesis offers a cleaner explanation: the Phillips curve was not dead, it was anaesthetised. China and global labour arbitrage put it in a coma. Domestic tightness in the labour market simply did not translate into wage inflation because firms could offshore production or threaten to. With that escape valve closing as global labour tightens, the relationship between domestic slack and domestic prices should reassert itself — particularly in services, which are far harder to offshore than goods.

This points to a bifurcated inflation picture that is already visible in the data. Manufactured goods, still subject to global competition and now to AI-driven productivity, can continue to see disinflation or outright deflation. But domestically produced services — healthcare, education, hospitality, personal care, construction — are labour-intensive, hard to trade across borders, and increasingly subject to wage pressure as workers grow scarce. Services inflation has proven the stickiest component of the post-2021 episode, and the demographic logic suggests that is structural, not transitory.

Baumol's Cost Disease Meets an Ageing Population

The services problem has a name in economics: Baumol's cost disease, after William Baumol's famous string-quartet illustration. It takes exactly as many musicians exactly as long to perform a Beethoven quartet today as it did in 1820 — there has been zero productivity growth in the activity itself. Yet those musicians' wages have risen enormously over two centuries, because they must be paid roughly in line with workers in sectors where productivity has soared. The cost of labour-intensive services therefore rises relentlessly relative to manufactured goods, even with no improvement in the service.

Healthcare and education are the canonical modern examples, and both have run inflation well above the headline rate for decades. Now overlay an ageing population. The sectors most resistant to productivity gains — elder care, nursing, medical services — are precisely the sectors where demand is exploding as the population greys. You are pouring rising demand into the part of the economy least able to expand supply through technology. Baumol's disease, which was a slow chronic drag in the disinflationary era, becomes an acute and compounding source of structural inflation when demographics turn.

Geopolitics as the Catalyst

Structural forces set the trajectory; shocks set the timing. And the geopolitical environment has become a recurring source of supply-side shocks in a way it had not been since the 1970s. The most consequential recent example was the disruption around the Strait of Hormuz in early 2025, following the escalation between the United States, Israel, and Iran. The Strait is the single most important chokepoint in global energy markets — on the order of a fifth of the world's oil and a comparable share of liquefied natural gas pass through a waterway only a few miles wide at its navigable channel. Even a partial, temporary disruption sent Brent crude sharply higher, with prices spiking well above $125 a barrel at the peak of the panic.

The point is not that any single oil spike is permanent — they rarely are. The point is that energy and commodity shocks now land on top of an economy whose underlying inflation is already structurally firmer, whose labour market is already tight, and whose inflation expectations are already less firmly anchored than they were in 2015. A shock that would have been absorbed quietly in the disinflationary era now risks feeding into wages and expectations, because the buffers that used to absorb it — slack labour, credible central banks, abundant supply — have all thinned. The era of reliable globalisation has given way to friend-shoring, reshoring, tariffs, and strategic stockpiling, all of which raise costs by design.

The Case for Persistently Higher Inflation

This is not a fringe view. In January 2025, Adam Posen and Peter Orszag — serious, mainstream voices, not perma-bears — laid out at the Peterson Institute for International Economics a credible path to inflation settling around 4% rather than the Federal Reserve's 2% target. Their argument stacked several of the forces discussed here: tariff pass-through raising goods prices, a structurally tight labour market limiting the disinflation that slack would normally provide, federal deficits running above 7% of GDP injecting demand, and — most dangerously — inflation expectations that have begun to drift rather than stay anchored at target.

Four percent does not sound dramatic next to the 9% peak of 2022. But it is a profound regime change. It would mean the Fed persistently missing its target to the upside, real returns on nominal bonds eroding year after year, and the compounding loss of purchasing power that turns a "manageable" inflation rate into a serious wealth transfer over a decade. A 2% world and a 4% world are not minor variations on a theme — they imply different bond yields, different equity valuations, different mortgage rates, and a different social contract.

Housing: Structural Inflation Hiding in Plain Sight

One of the most underappreciated structural pressures sits inside the inflation basket itself. Shelter accounts for roughly a third of the US Consumer Price Index — by far its largest single component. And the US housing market has developed a structural pathology that resists the normal cooling mechanism of higher interest rates: the mortgage lock-in effect.

During the era of near-zero rates, an enormous share of American homeowners refinanced into 30-year fixed mortgages at 3% to 4%. When the Fed then raised rates and prevailing mortgage rates climbed to 6% to 7%, those homeowners faced a brutal disincentive to move: selling would mean surrendering a sub-4% loan and taking on a new one at nearly double the rate. The overwhelming majority of outstanding mortgages are locked in well below current market rates. The result is a frozen market. Existing-home supply collapses because no one wants to sell, which keeps prices elevated even as transaction volumes fall. Higher rates, which are supposed to cool housing, paradoxically choke off supply and support prices.

So the largest component of CPI has become structurally sticky, partly insulated from monetary policy, and underpinned by a genuine long-run housing shortage in many developed economies. That is structural inflation hiding in plain sight, and it is one reason core inflation has been so reluctant to return cleanly to target.

The Cycle of Selective Inattention

Why do markets and policymakers keep underestimating this? Part of the answer is behavioural, and recent research formalises it. A 2025 NBER working paper by Olivier Coibion, Yuriy Gorodnichenko and co-authors (NBER WP 33858) documents how household and firm attention to inflation is not constant — it switches on sharply when inflation is high and salient, then fades to near-indifference once it recedes. People economise on attention; inflation is costly to track, so they ignore it until it hurts.

This "selective inattention" is dangerous because it mirrors the dynamics of the 1970s. In the early part of that decade, inflation was repeatedly dismissed as transitory and driven by one-off shocks — until expectations de-anchored and a wage-price spiral set in. The comfort of low attention lets inflation build a head of steam before the public and policymakers fully re-engage. If attention is once again fading after the 2021–23 spike while the structural pressures remain, the conditions for a repeat of that pattern are quietly assembling. Anchored expectations are a psychological state, not a law of nature, and they can shift faster than models assume.

Fiscal Dominance and the Unanchored Central Banker

This is the heart of the thesis and the source of its name. The phrase "the unanchored central banker" captures a regime in which the central bank is no longer the decisive force setting inflation — the government's fiscal position is. Economists call this fiscal dominance: when public debt and deficits grow so large that the central bank's freedom to fight inflation with higher rates is constrained by the damage those rates would do to the government's finances.

The arithmetic is stark. The US federal deficit has been running on the order of $1.7 to $1.8 trillion a year, with total federal debt now well into the tens of trillions and interest costs rising toward — and in some measures past — what the country spends on defence. When debt is that large, every percentage point of additional interest rate translates into hundreds of billions of dollars of extra interest expense. At some threshold, the political and financial cost of raising rates to crush inflation becomes prohibitive. The central bank, in effect, loses the argument to the government. It may be forced to tolerate inflation it would prefer to fight, because the alternative — a debt-servicing crisis — is worse. That is an unanchored central banker: institutionally independent on paper, but fiscally captured in practice.

In a fiscal-dominance regime, inflation becomes the path of least resistance. It quietly erodes the real value of the debt — a stealth default by another name — and it is politically easier than spending cuts or tax rises. History is full of governments that inflated away debt burdens because every other option was harder. The structural inflation forces described above do not need a conspiracy to take hold; they only need a central bank that flinches.

The Borrowing-Maturity Trap

A concrete symptom of this bind is visible in how the US Treasury is funding itself. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, before taking office, was sharply critical of the previous administration's reliance on short-term Treasury bills rather than locking in longer-term debt — arguing it was a short-sighted gamble that papered over the deficit and exposed the government to rollover risk. In office, the temptation to do exactly the same thing is enormous, because issuing short-term debt is cheaper in the moment and avoids locking in today's higher long-term yields.

The trap is that short-term funding has to be rolled over constantly. A government heavily dependent on short-dated bills must refinance a vast stock of debt every few months at whatever rate prevails. If inflation forces yields higher — or if markets simply demand more compensation to hold the debt of a heavily indebted sovereign — the interest bill reprices almost immediately across the whole short-term stack. That is rollover risk, and it is precisely the mechanism that converts a fiscal problem into an inflation problem: the more the debt is concentrated at the short end, the more the government needs rates kept low, and the more pressure the central bank faces to oblige.

The Counter-Argument: Could Technology Save Us?

Intellectual honesty requires taking the optimistic case seriously. The most powerful counter-argument is technological: artificial intelligence and automation could deliver a productivity surge large enough to offset shrinking labour supply. If a smaller workforce becomes dramatically more productive, output need not fall and inflationary pressure could be contained. This is a real possibility, and it would be foolish to dismiss it.

But two caveats matter. First, the sequence runs through inflation before disinflation. Building the AI economy requires an enormous capital expenditure boom — data centres, power generation, grid upgrades, semiconductors, skilled labour — all of which is inflationary in the near term, competing for scarce resources before any productivity payoff arrives. The picks-and-shovels build-out comes first; the disinflation comes later, if it comes at all on the timeline that matters.

Second, the productivity gains have to land in the right sectors. Baumol's disease is concentrated in labour-intensive services — and adoption there is slow. Japan, the world's most aged society and the country with the most urgent need for it, has been deploying healthcare and elder-care robotics for years, yet adoption rates remain in the low single digits of facilities. If even Japan, with every incentive to automate care, struggles to do so at scale, the assumption that AI will quickly neutralise services inflation in ageing societies looks optimistic. Technology may eventually win. It is unlikely to win fast enough to keep the next decade looking like the last one.

What the Public Already Senses

There is a telling gap between Wall Street and Main Street. Equity indices have spent much of the past year near record highs, pricing a soft landing and a return to benign conditions. Yet the University of Michigan's consumer sentiment index fell to around 47.6 in April 2026 — among the lowest readings in the survey's history, worse than the depths of the 2008 financial crisis. That is an extraordinary divergence.

Sentiment surveys capture something the aggregates miss: the lived experience of households for whom the price level — not just the rate of change — is what matters. Prices that rose 20-odd percent cumulatively since 2020 do not come back down when the inflation rate falls; they simply rise more slowly from a permanently higher base. The public's pessimism may be a more accurate read on the structural inflation regime than the market's optimism. People can feel the erosion in their purchasing power even when the official rate looks tame, and that lived erosion is exactly what feeds into wage demands and de-anchoring expectations.

Conclusion: Plan for a Different Regime

The argument here is not that hyperinflation is coming, nor that central banks are powerless. It is more measured and, in some ways, more consequential: the extraordinary disinflation of 1990 to 2020 was substantially a demographic accident, that accident is now reversing, and the structural forces lined up behind higher inflation — ageing populations, the return of the Phillips curve, Baumol's cost disease, sticky shelter costs, geopolitical supply shocks, and above all fiscal dominance — are deeper and more durable than a single rate cycle can address.

For investors, the implication is to hold lightly the assumption that inflation reliably returns to 2% and that bonds reliably hedge equities. A world that settles closer to 4%, punctuated by supply shocks and underpinned by a fiscally constrained central bank, calls for genuine inflation protection — real assets, shorter duration where appropriate, and exposure to the picks-and-shovels of the capital-expenditure boom — rather than a portfolio built for the regime that just ended. The central bankers of the last thirty years were, in a real sense, lucky. Betting that the luck continues is not a strategy.
