01 Strategy Overview

The Quant Fund is built the way a quant desk at a multi-strategy fund would build it: the objective is not the highest average return, but the highest compounded return per unit of risk, net of trading costs, with the left tail deliberately truncated. Long-run wealth is governed by the geometric growth rate, g ≈ μ − σ²/2, the "volatility tax" or variance drain, so a strategy that cuts volatility and avoids deep drawdowns without sacrificing expected return mechanically compounds faster.

Outperformance is engineered from four sources working together: (1) a basket of diversified, independently-replicated return premia rather than one fragile bet; (2) a machine-learning layer that blends those signals non-linearly; (3) a risk-allocation and volatility-targeting engine that defeats the volatility tax on compounding; and (4) a convex options overlay that protects the portfolio precisely when correlations go to one. Critically, the real edge is discipline, every signal is validated with purged cross-validation, deflated for the number of trials, and modelled net of costs and capacity. Markets' edges are small and decay; stacking many small, low-correlation, cost-survivable edges is what gives a credible path to beating the benchmark, not a single "genius" signal.

02 The Engine, six building blocks

01 Multi-factor premia Long/short value & momentum (Asness–Moskowitz–Pedersen 2013), quality/profitability (Novy-Marx 2013; QMJ) and low-beta (Frazzini–Pedersen 2014), filtered for multiple-testing (Harvey–Liu–Zhu 2016) and net-of-cost survival.
02 Trend & carry Cross-asset time-series momentum (Moskowitz–Ooi–Pedersen 2012; "A Century of Evidence" 2017) for crisis alpha, plus carry across bonds, FX, commodities and equities (Koijen–Moskowitz–Pedersen–Vrugt 2018).
03 Machine-learning blend Signals are combined by a regularised ML layer, gradient-boosted trees and shallow nets (Gu–Kelly–Xiu, RFS 2020), used only as an ensemble blender of economically-motivated features, never a black box.
04 Risk parity (HRP) Capital is allocated by risk contribution via Hierarchical Risk Parity (López de Prado 2016) on a Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage covariance, no unstable matrix inversion, far more stable out-of-sample than mean-variance.
05 Volatility targeting Gross exposure scales inversely to realised volatility to hold a constant ~10% portfolio vol (Moreira–Muir, JF 2017), raising the realised Sharpe and directly cutting the σ²/2 drag on compounding.
06 Options overlay Sell the variance-risk premium with defined-risk index options (Carr–Wu 2009), barbelled with standing out-of-the-money puts, short vol for carry, long the tail for crash convexity.

06 Why the Edge Should Persist

The real moat is anti-overfitting

Most published "market-beating" strategies are statistical mirages. Our defence is the part that actually matters: signals are validated with purged and combinatorial cross-validation to prevent look-ahead leakage, and every candidate strategy's Sharpe is adjusted by the Deflated Sharpe Ratio for the number of trials run (Bailey & López de Prado 2014; Advances in Financial Machine Learning 2018). Returns are simulated net of realistic transaction costs, market impact and capacity (Frazzini, Israel & Moskowitz 2018). A thin, fragile backtest is rejected even if its in-sample numbers look spectacular.

Compounding Defeating the volatility tax

Two books with the same average return but different volatility compound to very different terminal wealth, and a single deep drawdown is the hardest thing to recover from (a −50% loss needs +100% to break even). Volatility targeting de-risks into turbulence, raising the realised Sharpe and shrinking the σ²/2 drag; disciplined monthly rebalancing of volatile, low-correlation sleeves harvests a structural diversification / rebalancing return (Willenbrock 2011) that partially offsets the drain for free. Position sizing follows a fractional-Kelly rule (Kelly 1956; Thorp), large enough to compound, small enough that ruin is effectively impossible.

Diversification Many small, low-correlation edges

Value and momentum are negatively correlated, so combining them raises the Sharpe of the equity sleeve more than either alone; trend and carry are largely uncorrelated with the equity factors and tend to pay in exactly the environments a 60/40 is bleeding. Allocating by Hierarchical Risk Parity rather than dollars means no single bet dominates, and the covariance is shrunk (Ledoit–Wolf 2004) so the weights are stable out-of-sample rather than chasing estimation noise.

Convexity Owning the tail

Index implied volatility has, on average, exceeded subsequent realised volatility, a persistent variance-risk premium (Carr & Wu 2009; Bakshi & Kapadia 2003). The fund harvests it through small, defined-risk index option writing, then barbells that carry against a standing allocation to out-of-the-money puts and long-volatility convexity. The result is an asymmetric payoff that truncates the left tail rather than naïve, ungated premium selling, because in compounding, avoiding the deep loss matters more than capturing the last basis point of carry.

In short How it is meant to beat the benchmark

Many small, independently-documented, low-correlation edges (factors, trend, carry, the variance-risk premium) are blended by machine learning, allocated by risk so no single bet dominates, scaled to constant volatility to protect compounding, and wrapped in convexity so crashes don't reset the geometric return. The aim is a materially higher Sharpe and a shallower maximum drawdown than a 60/40, which, compounded over time and net of costs, is what "beating the market" actually means.
