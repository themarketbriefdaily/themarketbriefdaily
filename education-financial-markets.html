Financial Markets

1. Market architecture and participants

Roles of issuers, brokers, dealers, exchanges, buy-side institutions, and regulators in capital allocation.

Learning outcomes

  • Distinguish agency vs principal intermediation.
  • Map information, inventory, and counterparty risk across participant types.
  • Evaluate how venue design affects execution quality.

2. Primary vs secondary markets

Issuance process, underwriting economics, and how secondary liquidity feeds issuer cost of capital.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain the financing role of IPOs, follow-ons, and bond issuance.
  • Link liquidity premia to required return and valuation multiples.
  • Assess syndication and allocation trade-offs.

3. Order types and execution quality

Market, limit, stop, hidden, and algorithmic order routing under best-execution constraints.

Learning outcomes

  • Compute quoted/effective spread and implementation shortfall conceptually.
  • Choose order types by urgency and price certainty.
  • Identify execution-quality distortions during high volatility.

4. Price discovery and liquidity

Depth, resilience, immediacy, and adverse selection as core microstructure dimensions.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain how order-book depth translates into market impact.
  • Interpret liquidity regimes as state-dependent, not constant.
  • Apply capacity discipline to strategy evaluation.

5. Dealer balance sheets and market-making

Inventory risk, capital constraints, and balance-sheet transmission into spreads and depth.

Learning outcomes

  • Describe why dealer constraints widen spreads in stress.
  • Connect funding costs to intermediation capacity.
  • Assess liquidity fragility in dealer-centric markets.

6. Auction markets and benchmark closes

Opening/closing auctions, benchmark tracking, and order-imbalance risk management.

Learning outcomes

  • Assess when auction execution improves benchmark alignment.
  • Recognize crowding and imbalance risks around closes.
  • Compare auction vs continuous matching for large flow.

7. Clearing, settlement, and CCP risk

Post-trade infrastructure, variation margin, and resilience concentration in CCPs.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain netting and collateral mechanics.
  • Evaluate procyclical margin dynamics.
  • Identify operational and concentration risk in clearing systems.

8. Repo, collateral, and funding stress

Funding liquidity as a driver of market liquidity and cross-asset volatility transmission.

Learning outcomes

  • Link haircut changes to market depth deterioration.
  • Interpret collateral quality and rehypothecation effects.
  • Incorporate funding stress scenarios in risk governance.

9. Regulation, transparency, and integrity

Disclosure, surveillance, conduct standards, and market trust premia.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain why governance quality lowers participation frictions.
  • Assess investor-protection measures and market quality.
  • Integrate integrity risks into market-selection decisions.

10. Crisis case studies and policy lessons

GFC, gilt/liability-driven episodes, and post-crisis reforms.

Learning outcomes

  • Diagnose common stress pathways: leverage, liquidity mismatch, collateral calls.
  • Compare policy interventions and trade-offs.
  • Translate crisis evidence into implementation rules.

Core references

  • BIS Quarterly Reviews and market-functioning papers.
  • CPMI-IOSCO guidance on CCP resilience and margining.
  • CFA Program readings on market organization and trading.
  • Harris, L. (Trading and Exchanges).
  • FCA market structure and conduct publications.
